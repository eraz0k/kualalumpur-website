Jalan Alor

Jalan Alor is a unique food destination in the heart of Kuala Lumpur. The whole character of the city changes when you step onto the street. Gone are the modern shopping malls that align the city, which are replaced with something of the past age. During the day, there is not much activity but when the sun goes down, the street will be hustling and bustling with activity.

One of the most famous stalls on Jalan Alor is Wong Ah Wah, renowned for its succulent grilled chicken wings. Another must-try is the char kway teow—flat rice noodles stir-fried with prawns, eggs, bean sprouts, and Chinese sausage. No visit to Jalan Alor is complete without trying the satay—skewers of marinated meat, usually chicken or beef, grilled to perfection and served with a rich, creamy peanut sauce. From giant prawns to tender squid and perfectly steamed fish, the seafood offerings on Jalan Alor are a testament to Malaysia's coastal bounty.

Jalan Alor isn't just about food, it's also about the atmosphere. As you walk down the street, you'll notice a plethora of vendors selling everything from fresh fruit to souvenirs. The vibrant night market scene adds another layer of excitement to the experience. It's a great place to pick up a unique gift or just enjoy some people-watching while you take a break from eating.

As Jalan Alor is located within the Bukit Bintang area, it can be easily found if you are staying somewhere in the city center. For example, Radium Palace is located 1 minute from the food street, from Capitol Hotel it is a 2 minute walk and 5-star Grand Millennium is only 10 minutes from Jalan Alor. If you are staying outside the city center, you can get to Jalan Alor by metro. Get out at Bukit Bintang Station (right next to Lot10 Shopping Mall). From there it is a 5 minute walk to Jalan Alor.

Petaling Street

A haggler's haven, the busy market stalls of Petaling Street are known as the birthplace of Kuala Lumpur. Beneath a vast green canopy, shoppers can get lost in endless stalls selling a wealth of faux-branded items and plenty of hot bites from stalls lining the street. The market's open until late at night, so you can while away the hours gorging on hokkien mee and perusing handbags of dubious origin. Petaling Street, Kuala Lumpur's Chinatown, is known to all Malaysians to be Kuala Lumpur's food haven. The hawker stalls have been dishing out mouthwatering delights for generations.

With large swathes of citizens from such culinarily colorful nations, Malaysian food is an explosion of flavors and spices. The capital, Kuala Lumpur, has among the most diverse ranges of food anywhere, with influences from the many Tamil, Arab, Malay, Indonesian, Thai, and European settlers who settled its shores. While almost 70% of the population in Malaysia is ethnically Malay, 23% are Chinese and 7% are Indian. Leaving the country without treating yourself to the incredible range of street food in Kuala Lumpur would be a grave injustice upon your taste buds.

Kim Soya Bean is one of the most popular street food stalls in Chinatown in Kuala Lumpur, known for its tau foo fah (RM2.20) - a soft and melt-in-your-mouth tofu served hot with white or brown sugar syrup with a hint of ginger. Sin Kiew Yee Shin Kee Beef Noodles is a must-visit in Petaling Street with its family-run joint serving tasty beef noodles (RM10), both dry and in soup. Order a mouthwatering bowl of hokkien mee (RM10) noodles stir-fried in a thick soy sauce gravy and served with prawns, crunchy pork, shrimp, cabbage, and lard fritters at Kim Lian Kee, established by an immigrant named Mr. Ong in Petaling Street Market. If you're looking for a Petaling Street dinner option, then head to Hong Kee Claypot Chicken Rice (RM15), situated near KK Mart.

The easiest and most recommended route to Petaling Street is to take the Kelana Jaya Line (Line 5) and alight at the Pasar Seni station. From the station, you are directly connected via a covered walkway to Central Market (Pasar Seni). From Central Market, it is a very short 3-5 minute walk to the main entrance arch of Petaling Street. You cannot get lost.

Pudu Food Street (Pudu Wai Sek Kai)

Pudu sits just south of Kuala Lumpur's city center, sandwiched between glitzy Bukit Bintang and the grittier, more local vibes of Chan Sow Lin. There are many streets in Kuala Lumpur that turn into an evening market called pasar malam. In the morning this street is called Jalan Sayur. In the evening it transforms into Glutton Street. Pudu is an old part of town in Kuala Lumpur and also houses the famous “Wai Sek Kei” or Glutton Street, which is much loved by locals and tourists alike for local hawker cuisine passed down for generations.

Once home to the infamous Pudu Prison—now long gone—this area has transformed into a buzzing hub of old-school markets, hidden alleyways, and some of the city's best street food. Unlike the polished streets of Bukit Bintang, Pudu keeps things gritty, with aging shophouses, hole-in-the-wall eateries, and the kind of unfiltered local life that makes KL special. Mornings are for the wet market, where vendors hawk everything from fresh seafood to knock-off electronics, while nights bring a whole different energy as hawker stalls take over the sidewalks.

The famous Tai Bu mee stall is run by Yong Jiun Liang, the third generation manning this family business on Pudu’s Wai Sek Kai, cooking up their signature Tai Bu mee with a comforting heft of homemade noodles freshly made every morning in the empty shop next door. The friendly brother-sister team that took over their father's tong sui stall has perfected the delicate art of egg steaming. Rock sugar and evaporated milk are added to the egg and steamed until silky and smooth like tofu. Staking out the corner lot to operate a family business that spans more than five decades, the trio of Choy siblings churn out plates of velvety flat rice noodle rolls soaked in spicy homemade curry sauce.

To get to Pudu Wai Sek Kai take the LRT Ampang (Line 3, Yellow) or LRT Sri Petaling (Line 4, Brown) to Pudu Station (AG10, SP10). Use the Jalan San Pang Exit (past the small convenience stores in the station). Walk along the long path under the elevated train track, but turn left at the end to go to Jalan Pudu instead of turning right to go to the carpark by Jalan San Pang. Do not use the Jalan Pudu Exit. It empties into an awkward and dark alley behind retail buildings.
